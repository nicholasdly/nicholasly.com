Although GPT and other large language models (LLMs) are extremely powerful, they are general
purpose. They were trained on millions of lines of text with the goal to understand human language,
and while they have succeeded with that, their vast amount of general knowledge is both a strength
and a weakness. If you were to ask GPT a question about your own information, notes, or product, it
will most likely answer incorrectly due to the fact that it has not been trained on that
information.

There are two primary ways to get around this: fine tuning the model, or utilizing context
injection. Both methods have their own unique pros and cons, but context injection is the popular
choice due to its ability to use updated data in realtime.

Building your own "ChatGPT" using your own information is pretty straightforward, and only involves
four core processes:

1. Parsing your knowledge base into subsections.
2. Generating and storing embeddings for each subsection.
3. Finding the most relevant subsections related to a query.
4. Communicating the relevant information to a model.

In this blog post, I'll be going over each step at a high level to help simplify the process. Don't
worry if you aren't a technical person, there won't be any code.

## Context injection vs. fine tuning

**Context injection**, or retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), is the process of
optimizing the output of an LLM so that it references an authoritative knowledge base outside of its
original training data.

More specifically, context injection is a strategy of prompt engineering that allows us to directly
"inject" contextual information into the prompt(s) we provide to a model, manipulating how it
responds to future questions.

**Fine tuning** is the process of taking a pretrained model, such as GPT-4, and further training it
on a smaller, targeted data set. This maintains the original capabilities of a pretrained model,
while adapting it to suit more specialized use cases.

Using context injection, you can specifically tell the model to respond using only the information
you provide it. This approach saves time and resources compared to fine tuning, since you do not
need to go through the tedious process of fine tuning your model every time there are changes to
your knowledge base.

## Parsing your knowledge base

> Your data is more important than the specific toolset.

Before you can jump into the more technical processes, you have to fully understand how you want to
break down your knowledge base. Your approach to parsing this information can differ significantly
depending on a variety of reasons, such as how your it is stored, where it is stored, how it is
formatted.

We could parse all of the data ourselves using techniques we come up with, but lets not reinvent the
wheel. There are an abundance of performant parsing tools and libraries, such as
[unified](https://unifiedjs.com/), that compiles Markdown into a syntax tree we can easily use to
process our data.

Plain text, Markdown, PDF, Microsoft Word documents, and more, can all be parsed in their own ways.
All that matters is that our data is structured so that it can be easily separated into sections
that generally isolate differing topics. Keeping different topics isolated is incredibly important
to optimize performance and accuracy, since each section will be represented as unique embeddings
and therefore will be searched upon by the semantics of the section.

## Generating embeddings

> Embeddings are a way to represent text, images, or other types of media as vectors, a form that
machine learning models can easily process.

Generating text embeddings is a straightforward process, and involves using an text embedding model.
There are a lot of options to choose from, all with varying performance. For example, a solid choice
would be OpenAI's third generation text embedding models, `text-embedding-3-small` and
`text-embedding-3-large`, which are both performant, generously priced, and have an easy to use API.

OpenAI's models will do perfectly fine for most people, and you can totally move on to the next
section. For those of you who are interested in a bit more detail, there are definitely more factors
to consider when choosing a embedding model that you may be interested in:

- Do you want to use a free or open source model?
- Do you want to self host your model?
- Do you want to avoid relying on someone else's API?
- Do you want to avoid vendor lock-in?
- Do you want to be able to use the model offline?
- Maybe you just want to use the absolute best performant model?

These are all great questions to ask yourself when building a project that depends on a text
embedding model, and while I can jump into the rabbit hole that is the embedding model ecosystem,
I highly recommend watching
[$0 Embeddings (OpenAI vs. free & open source)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QdDoFfkVkcw), a
technical video by [Greg Richardson](https://twitter.com/ggrdson).

## Storing and querying embeddings

Now that you have parsed your knowledge base into sections and converted each section to embeddings,
you have to store those embeddings somewhere. Again, there are lots of viable options, but a common
and performant choice would be a vector database. There are many paid database services, such as
[Supabase](https://supabase.com/vector), [Pinecone](https://www.pinecone.io/), and
[Upstash](https://upstash.com/), which provide an improved experience at a cost. You can also use
your own database.

[PostgreSQL](https://www.postgresql.org/) is an open source relational database that can be self
hosted at no cost. When combined with the [pgvector](https://github.com/pgvector/pgvector)
extension, you are able to store vectors and utilize various predefined algorithms to query the
database by semantic similarity.
